BRIEF · 01

What this workshop is for

Nobody runs this workshop to walk away with a few cool demos. The demos are a nice side effect. What I'm actually after is a mindset shift: by the end, every engineer in the room has moved a real distance from "AI is a faster autocomplete" toward "AI is how I work." Some of them already live at the far end with their own agent workflows. Others are still typing into the editor and tabbing for a completion. They're all in one room, and my job is to move the whole room to the right, not to entertain the people who are already there.

So I set one constraint that does most of the teaching for me: nobody writes code directly in the IDE — everything goes through AI. It's deliberately uncomfortable, and the discomfort is the point. Once you can't reach for the keyboard to "just fix this one line," you're forced into the thing this is really about: describing intent, engineering context, and steering a loop instead of producing keystrokes. What I want is for that constraint to feel possible and better, so people leave preferring it rather than enduring it.

I teach primarily through Claude Code, because it puts the full agentic toolkit — skills, subagents, hooks, MCP — in front of you as first-class, and because going deep on one tool beats going shallow on three. Anyone on Cursor or Codex still gets everything: I teach each pattern as a portable idea, and the Rosetta maps it back to their tool.

The honest framing I give the room: this is not a productivity-hack session. The four things I want you to walk out with — thinking in context instead of implementation, spending your attention on intent and architecture instead of typing, building workflows instead of writing code faster, and collaborating with the agent instead of treating it as autocomplete — aren't features you install. They're a change in where you point your attention.

I teach on one small, real reference app — a stand-in codebase scoped so a genuine end-to-end vertical slice fits inside a live demo. Swap in whatever app suits the group; the patterns don't care what it is.

Outcome

Every engineer can take a fuzzy goal, turn it into a sliced plan, and drive an AI agent through a verify-and-iterate loop to code they trust — without touching the editor themselves.

BRIEF · 03

The shape of the program

It runs in three movements, and I schedule them however the engagement allows. First the teaching: a sequence of short modules that each leave the room with one thing, climbing to a planning clinic where every team puts the ideas to work on its own real project. Then a stretch of self-guided build, where teams apply the workflow on their own with no facilitator in the room — the one-page self-guide is what carries them, and it does real load-bearing work. Then the coaching: reactive floor-work with no fixed agenda, where I move between teams and push each one further. The back half of this document is the playbook I run that from.

M0≈ 35 MIN

The ReframeWhy You're Not Allowed to Touch the Keyboard

Hands-on · Light
Objective

The room understands why the no-IDE rule exists and reframes it from punishment to training wheels, before a single tool appears.

I open with the uncomfortable rule stated plainly, then I make the case for it. The reason you reach into the editor to fix one line is that it's faster right now, and that local speed is exactly what keeps you from building the workflow that's faster every time after. Every time you take the keyboard back, you skip the step where you'd have learned to say what you wanted well enough for the agent to do it. The rule removes the escape hatch so the better habit has room to form.

Then I name the four shifts in plain terms: stop thinking about the implementation and start thinking about the context the agent needs; stop spending your best attention on typing and spend it on intent and architecture; stop trying to write code faster and start building a workflow that writes it for you; stop treating the agent as autocomplete and start working with it like a fast, literal, eager junior who never gets tired and never reads your mind. I don't demo anything yet — this module sells the why so everything after it runs downhill.

I close honestly with the skeptics and the already-advanced: yes, in here this will sometimes be slower than just typing the line. That's the cost I'm asking you to pay on purpose, to buy the habit. Hold that thought — M2 says exactly when, in real life, you should take the keyboard back.

What I cover
  • The no-IDE rule, stated and defended — local speed vs. compounding workflow speed
  • The four shifts, put in plain engineer terms
  • The "eager literal junior" mental model for what an agent actually is
  • A one-line promise: by the end you won't want the keyboard back
Facilitator notes

Keep this short and high-energy; the temptation is to over-explain. Read the room's skepticism and remember who frowned — those are the people I convert with the live build in M1. I don't open a terminal during M0; the contrast with M1 is the whole point.

Room leaves with

A reason to take the constraint seriously instead of gaming it.

M1≈ 55 MIN

The ProofBuilding a Feature Live, Through AI Only

Hands-on · Heavy (facilitator) · watch-only (room)
Objective

Kill the "this can't really work for real code" objection by shipping a working vertical slice of the reference app live, no editor, including a real recovery from a real mistake.

This is the centerpiece and the trust-builder. I pick one slice of the app small enough to finish and real enough that it can't be faked — the one I reach for is "add a 'save' button that persists a record": a button, an API endpoint, persistence, and a test that proves it round-trips. I build it entirely by steering the agent: I state the intent, let it propose a plan, approve or correct the plan, let it write the code, run the test, read the failure, feed the failure back, and watch it converge. I narrate my thinking out loud the whole time — what I'm deciding and why, never how to type it.

The most important 8 minutes of this module are the planted mistake. I give the agent a deliberately under-specified instruction early — omit a constraint, or let it guess a field name wrong — so it produces something subtly wrong. Then I don't fix it by hand. I show the room the loop catching it: the test goes red, I read the actual error, I feed it back with the missing context, the agent corrects itself. This is the entire workshop in one motion. The lesson isn't "the agent is perfect"; it's "the agent plus a verification loop plus you reading the output is a system that converges, and you never touched the keyboard."

I end by pointing at the artifact: a working, tested feature, built in under an hour, by someone who typed only prose. Then I say the quiet part out loud: that felt slower than typing it for the first two minutes and faster than typing it for the last twenty.

What I cover
  • A full intent → plan → act → verify → iterate cycle on real code, live
  • Reading agent output critically — the test result is the source of truth, not the agent's confidence
  • The planted-mistake recovery: how context, not correction-by-hand, fixes a wrong result
  • The feel of working in prose: where it drags, where it flies
Facilitator notes

Rehearse until the reference-app slice is muscle memory, then rehearse the recovery twice more — the demo gods punish the unrehearsed recovery. Keep a known-good transcript or branch as a fallback if a live run wedges. If the agent one-shots it cleanly, induce a mistake anyway — the recovery is non-negotiable teaching. Weave in a 60-second secrets-hygiene aside when you wire the endpoint.

Room leaves with

Firsthand proof that real, tested code ships through prose alone — and a picture of what "good" looks like before they try it.

M2≈ 45 MIN

LoopsYou Steer a Loop, You Don't Write Code

Hands-on · Light
Objective

Install the single mental model the whole no-IDE workshop rests on — the agentic loop — and, just as importantly, name when not to use it.

This is the "why" lecture, deliberately conceptual. Everything you saw in M1 was one loop: plan → act → observe → verify → repeat. The shift from autocomplete-thinking to AI-first thinking is the shift from expecting one good answer to running a loop that converges on one. Autocomplete is a single shot. An agent is a loop — you set a direction, it acts, something observable happens (a test runs, a file changes, an error prints), and that observation steers the next turn. Your job moves from producing the output to designing the loop and judging each turn.

I make the verification point concrete, because it's where beginners fail: a loop without a real observation at each turn isn't a loop, it's hopeful one-shotting in a trench coat. The observation has to be something true about the world — a passing or failing test, a type error, a running app, a diff you actually read — not the agent's own claim that it's done. Iteration plus honest verification beats one-shot prompting, every time.

The honest caveat — when the loop is the wrong tool

I treat this with the same seriousness as the rest — it's what earns the skeptics in the back row. AI-first is not "AI-only-always," and pretending otherwise burns credibility with the very engineers I most need to convert.

The change is smaller than the prompt to describe it.A one-character fix you can see is faster typed than explained. (In here you do it through AI anyway, to build the habit — but I say out loud that in real life this is a judgment call, not a commandment.)
You can't verify the output and the cost of wrong is high.Security-sensitive code, money-moving logic, irreversible migrations — if you don't have a loop that can prove the result, the agent's confidence is worth nothing. Slow down and review by hand.
The agent is going in circles.Two or three turns with no progress is a signal, not a reason to prompt harder. Usually the context is wrong or the task isn't sliced small enough — stop, fix the setup, restart. You'll see this constantly once teams are building on their own.

I land the verdict cleanly: the loop is the default, not the law. An AI-first engineer runs the loop by reflex and knows its three failure cases by heart.

What I cover
  • The agentic loop — plan → act → observe → verify → repeat — as the core model
  • Why verification is the load-bearing step, and what counts as a real observation
  • One-shot vs. loop, and why autocomplete habits quietly sabotage agent work
  • The three cases where the loop is the wrong tool — said honestly
Facilitator notes

Anchor every abstract point to a concrete moment from M1 ("remember when the test went red — that red was the observation"). The caveat is your credibility deposit with advanced engineers; do not soften it into "but AI is still always great." Name the failure cases with conviction.

Room leaves with

A model they can apply to any task — and permission to distrust the agent in the cases where distrust is correct.

M3≈ 50 MIN

From PRD to a Sliced BoardFeeding the Loop Well

Hands-on · Medium-light
Objective

Teach the discipline that makes the loop work — writing a tight PRD with the agent, then cutting it into thin vertical slices small enough to each be one good loop.

A loop is only as good as what you feed it, and the most common reason an agent flails is being handed a vague mountain instead of a sharp small task. I demo writing a short PRD with the agent for a feature on the reference app — not a novel, a tight spec: the problem, the user-visible behavior, the acceptance criteria, the explicit non-goals. The non-goals matter as much as the goals; they're how you stop the agent from gold-plating.

Then the heart of it: vertical slicing, which is just tracer-bullet thinking from The Pragmatic Programmer applied to AI work. A bad task list is horizontal — "build the data layer," "build the API," "build the UI" — three tasks where nothing works until all three are done. A good task list is vertical — each card is a thin slice that goes all the way through (a button that calls a real endpoint that writes a real row that a real test checks), delivers something demoable, and can be built and verified in a single loop. I cut the app's "save a record" feature into vertical slices live: the first slice is ugly and end-to-end and works; later slices add width, not depth.

I show the tooling lightly: the agent can do the slicing with you (the write-a-prd and prd-to-issues/to-issues flows turn a PRD into tracer-bullet tasks), and each slice lands as a card on whatever board the team already uses — GitHub issues, a markdown checklist, a sticky wall. I teach the discipline, not the board.

What I cover
  • Writing a tight PRD with the agent — behavior, acceptance criteria, and explicit non-goals
  • Vertical slice vs. horizontal layer — the tracer-bullet principle, shown on the reference app
  • What makes a good slice: thin, end-to-end, demoable, with verification built in
  • Turning a PRD into a board of sliced cards (write-a-prd, prd-to-issues), mapped to the team's own board
Facilitator notes

The slicing demo is the keeper — spend your time there, not on PRD formatting. The "first slice is ugly and works" moment is the one to dwell on; engineers trained on tidy architecture resist it. Tie it straight back to M2: each card is one turn of the loop, sized so the loop can actually close.

Room leaves with

The ability to take a feature and cut it into tasks an agent can actually finish one at a time.

① Trigger · how it's invoked

Two ways in — pick on purpose

User-invoked

  • Only you fire it, by name
  • No description in the agent's context
  • Cost: cognitive load — you must remember it exists

Model-invoked

  • The agent fires it itself (and you can too)
  • Description sits in context every turn
  • Cost: context load + it may not fire when it should

Our endpoint skill → model-invoked. We want the agent to reach for it automatically. So the description has to earn its keep.

Talk track

"Two famous libraries bet opposite ways. Superpowers is mostly model-invoked — flexible. Matt Pocock's are mostly user-invoked — he types the name, removing the risk the agent just doesn't fire it, which you'd otherwise have to eval away."

"Rule: model-invoke only when the agent must reach it on its own. Every model-invoked skill taxes every request your whole team makes."

say "To make one user-invoked instead: disable-model-invocation: true — the description leaves the agent's view."

M1 · PREP

The Live-Build SpecRehearse to Muscle Memory

Repo: the reference app (the shared playground), checked out clean on a known-good commit.

The slice to build live: "Save this record." A user viewing a record clicks Save; it persists; reloading shows it; a test proves the round-trip. Thin, end-to-end, demoable — a textbook vertical slice, and a silent preview of M3.

The loop to demonstrate, out loud
  1. Intent — state the slice and its acceptance criteria as prose. No implementation talk.
  2. Plan — let the agent propose the plan (endpoint, persistence, UI hook, test). Approve or correct it as a plan, before any code.
  3. Act — agent writes the code across the layers.
  4. Observe — run the test. The observation is the result, not the agent's say-so.
  5. The planted mistake — earlier, under-specify one constraint (omit an owner id, or let it guess the persistence field name). The test goes red here.
  6. Recover by context, not by hand — read the real error aloud, feed back the missing constraint, let the agent fix itself, re-run to green.
  7. Land it — point at the working, tested feature built in prose; deliver the "slower for two minutes, faster for twenty" line.
Fallbacks

A pre-recorded transcript or a finished branch in case a live run wedges; a second rehearsed mistake in case the first one self-resolves. Never let the recovery be the unrehearsed part. Woven asides (≈60s each): secrets hygiene when wiring the endpoint; "the test is the truth, not the agent's confidence" when it goes green.

COACHING · 01

Reactive floor-coachingHow to Run It

Not a timed agenda. This is reactive floor-coaching: I move between teams, challenge assumptions, and nudge toward better workflow — solo, without starving the quiet teams.

Work in deliberate laps, not by following the noise. Reactive coaching skews to the loudest, most-stuck, or most-confident teams; a visible checklist of every team, ticked each lap, is the fix. Touch every team early, before you go deep with anyone — a fast diagnostic lap first, then depth where it's needed. Then cycle: diagnose, intervene, leave them with one concrete next move, come back to verify it landed.

Stance

Challenge, don't solve. The goal is a mindset shift, which doesn't happen if you fix their loop for them. Ask the question that makes them see the better move; make them run it. You are coaching the workflow, not debugging the project.

Say"Let it finish the turn. What's the observation you're going to check — is there a test? Let's make the loop close on its own."
No plan, straight to code +
a huge vague prompt, agent off building something sprawling
Say"Stop — what's the thinnest slice that proves this works end-to-end? Let's get a plan you approve before it writes anything." Invoke plan mode in front of them.
Context starvation +
agent guessing field names, ignoring conventions, repeating mistakes
Say"What does it actually know about your repo? Show me your CLAUDE.md." Usually it's empty. Fix it together, watch the next loop improve.
One-shotting +
prompt, take output, discard, re-prompt from scratch — autocomplete habits
Say"You're throwing away the loop. Don't restart — feed the result back. What did this turn get wrong, specifically?"
Accepting unverified output +
"it says it's done," no test run, moving on
Say"How do you know it works? The agent's confidence isn't evidence. What would convince you before a real deploy?"
Sneaking hand-fixes +
someone's in the editor — the rule is broken
Say (light touch, not police)"I get it, it's faster right now. But you just skipped the part where you'd learn to say what you wanted. Put it back through the agent — what context was it missing that made you want to fix it yourself?"
A stuck loop nobody stops +
turn after turn, no progress, rising frustration
Say"Two turns, no progress — that's a signal. The context is wrong or the slice is too big. Stop the loop, fix the setup, restart."
The disengaged advanced engineer +
already AI-first, bored, coasting
Stretch, don't bore"Your loop's clean. Now: can you make this a skill your team reuses? Can a subagent parallelize it? Show me something I haven't seen today." Then pull others over to learn from them.

The coverage sweep

Twice across the session, do a fast silent audit: have I given every team real time? The teams that don't flag you down are the ones quietly getting the least — the over-confident team heading the wrong way and the under-confident team too polite to interrupt. Go to them on purpose.

Closing

End with a short reflection mirroring the one that closed the teaching — what shifted since then? Listen for the autocomplete-users describing loops and slices in their own words; that's the KPI moving, and what the org wants to hear. Flag for the sponsor (privately) which teams moved most and where the resistance still sits.

REF · LOGISTICS

How I run it & the known trade-offs

Format. The teaching is hands-on by design once the clinic starts — the lectures are tight teach-and-demo, and the clinic is where the room's hands are on the keyboard. The coaching is entirely hands-on for the teams; I coach, I never take the keyboard. I deliver in Hebrew; this document is the English run-of-show.

Solo delivery. I run it all alone. The clinic and the coaching both depend on the coverage discipline — deliberate laps, time-boxed stops — to make one facilitator reach every team.

Three trade-offs built in, and how I compensate
  1. Same content for everyone, no tiering. Advanced engineers risk boredom. Counter with genuine depth (the M2 caveat, the M4 description craft) and the no-IDE discipline, which humbles even the fluent — and in the coaching, redirect bored experts into skills/subagents stretch work and deputize them to help peers.
  2. Teaching hands-on is on the shared reference app; the first transfer to their own project is the clinic and then the self-guided build — when I'm away. That's why the self-guide carries real weight; walk the room through it at M6.
  3. The coaching is reactive-only. Coverage skews to the loud. The coverage sweep is the explicit fix — audit twice, go to the quiet teams on purpose.
